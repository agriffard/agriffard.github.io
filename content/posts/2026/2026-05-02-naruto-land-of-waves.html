After the bell test, Team 7 gets its first mission outside the village — and Naruto volume 2 delivers its second lesson in team 7. A mission announced as C-rank (routine escort) turns out to be S-rank with a rogue jōnin, Zabuza. The team is under-resourced for the actual problem. Sound familiar?

Scope creep has always existed

Tazuna lied about the nature of the mission due to budget constraints. How many projects start as "a small showcase website" and become a multitenant platform with RBAC?

The team must decide: abandon (contractually legitimate) or continue. Kakashi consults his team instead of deciding alone, and they choose to continue with full knowledge of the risks. That's a transparent contract renegotiation, not denial of the problem.

Complementarity beats raw talent

Against Zabuza, it's the improvised Naruto/Sasuke duo — the transformed-shuriken trick — that frees Kakashi. Naruto isn't the strongest, but his creativity unlocks the situation.

In a dev team: the senior architect can be "imprisoned" in a problem (the water prison = tunnel vision on a bug), and sometimes it's the lateral idea from a less experienced profile that unblocks everything.

A leader who shows his limits strengthens the team

Kakashi collapses from exhaustion after the fight (Sharingan overuse). He's not invincible, and the team must learn to function without him — hence the tree-climbing training.

That's exactly the bus factor: a tech lead who does everything himself creates a fragile team. Kakashi's right reflex is to upskill his team — chakra control, meaning fundamentals, not flashy techniques — rather than carrying everything.

Growth comes through boring fundamentals

Walking up trees is nothing spectacular, but it's the prerequisite for everything else. The dev equivalent: tests, naming, code review, Git mastery. Not glamorous, but it's what lets you hold up in the "real fight."

The enemy can become a mirror

Haku and Zabuza show another form of "team" relationship: the tool serving the master, loyalty without healthy reciprocity. It's the anti-model of Team 7 — and the anti-model of management that treats devs as interchangeable resources. Zabuza cries at the end because he realizes too late the value of human connection.

Conclusion

The common thread of the first two volumes fits in one Kakashi-style sentence: a team isn't the sum of three individuals working side by side — it's an entity that protects its members and compensates for each one's weaknesses.
