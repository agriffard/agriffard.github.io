![Too busy to try the wheel](/assets/blog/TooBusy.jpg)

There's a cartoon that says more about technology adoption than most conference talks.

On the left, a caveman holds out two **round wheels**. On the right, two others are **dragging a heavy square box** across the ground, exhausted, leaning into the effort. One of them waves a hand — *"No thanks!"* — and the other pants, *"We are too busy."*

Too busy doing the hard thing to try the thing that would make it easy.

It's funny because it's familiar. And in 2026, the box is full of code and the wheel is called **AI**.

## 1. The square box is the status quo

The cavemen aren't lazy. Look at them — they're working *hard*. They sweat, they strain, they make progress. The square box moves.

That's the trap. The square box **works**. It just works badly. And because it works, there's never an obvious moment to stop and reconsider it.

In software teams the square box is everything we drag by hand because we always have:

- writing the same boilerplate for the hundredth time
- copy-pasting a function and adapting it instead of looking for the abstraction
- manually wiring tests we could have generated
- reading three pages of docs to find one method signature

None of it is *broken*. All of it is **draggable**. So we drag.

## 2. "We are too busy" is the real obstacle

Notice the reason given. Not *"the wheel won't work"*. Not *"we tried it and it failed"*. The reason is **"we are too busy."**

The workload itself becomes the excuse not to fix the workload. This is the most human and most expensive form of resistance to change — and it's exactly what AI adoption runs into.

> "I don't have time to learn the agent, I have deliverables."  
> "I'll look at it after this sprint."  
> "Faster to just write it myself."

Each sentence is locally true and globally fatal. The box keeps moving, so the wheel keeps waiting.

## 3. The wheel always looks like a toy at first

Here's the other half of the joke: the guy with the wheels isn't dragging anything. From the box-pullers' point of view, **he's not even working**. He's standing around holding two useless discs while they do the real labor.

Early AI tools got the same look. *Autocomplete on steroids. A toy. Cute demo, won't survive real code.* And honestly — early on, sometimes that was fair.

But the wheel doesn't have to be perfect. It only has to be **better than dragging**. The first wheel was probably lopsided too. It still beat a square.

## 4. Adoption isn't about the tool, it's about stopping

The cavemen don't need a better wheel. They need to **put the box down for five minutes**.

That's the whole problem with AI adoption. The blocker is almost never the technology — it's carving out the time to change posture while the work keeps coming. Stopping feels like falling behind. It's the only way to catch up.

The teams that adopt AI well aren't the ones with the smartest engineers. They're the ones who **scheduled the pause**:

- a sprint where shipping less is allowed, on purpose
- time to fail with the new tool on a low-stakes task
- permission to be slow before being fast

## Conclusion: put the box down

Twenty years into this craft, I've been every character in that cartoon. The one dragging the box because deadlines. The one saying "no thanks" because the new thing felt like a toy. And — eventually — the one holding the wheel, wondering why nobody wants to stop for five minutes.

The wheel doesn't win because it's clever. It wins because, one day, somebody is tired enough to put the box down and try.

> The cavemen who picked up the wheel didn't work harder.  
> They just stopped dragging.
