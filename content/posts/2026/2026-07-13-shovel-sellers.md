Imagine. You have been digging with a shovel and a pickaxe for years. You know your terrain, your tools, your moves. It is tiring, sometimes slow, but it works.

One day, someone knocks on your door: "Good news! Here is a new method and new tools that will save you considerable time and spare you a lot of effort."

Wonderful, isn't it?

## The deal of the century

No downsides, they tell you. Well, almost.

**You have to rent the machine.** You could also buy it, but that is much more expensive. And frankly, it would be a shame: next month, the new model comes out. More powerful, faster, smarter. The one you just acquired will already be outdated. Renting is more reasonable. A monthly subscription, no commitment (or almost).

**You have to pay for the energy.** The machine does not run on the sweat of your brow, but it consumes. A lot. Every scoop of the bucket has a price, billed per unit. The more you dig, the more you pay. And if you dig fast and deep, the bill follows the same curve.

**You have to take a training course.** Because these tools are complex, you see. Using them naively is a waste. To get the best out of them, you had better pay a specialized trainer, certified in the art of handling the lever and steering the bucket. Count on a few days of training, renewable with each new model — since, remember, there is one every month.

## Let's do the math

Let's recap what this revolution with "no downsides" actually costs:

| Item | Before | After |
|------|--------|-------|
| Tool | A shovel, bought once | A monthly subscription, forever |
| Energy | Your arms | A metered bill |
| Skill | Your experience | A paid training course, to be renewed |
| Obsolescence | A shovel lasts 20 years | A model lasts 1 month |

Of course, the machine digs faster. Nobody disputes that. That is not the question.

The question is: **who makes money in this story?**

## The gold rush

During the gold rush, it was not the gold diggers who made a fortune. Most of them went home empty-handed. The ones who got rich were the sellers of shovels, pickaxes, pans, and reinforced jeans.

Today, history repeats itself with unsettling precision:

- The **shovel sellers** collect the subscriptions and the energy tokens.
- The **training sellers** monetize their expertise on tools that change every month — a guarantee of captive customers.
- And the **diggers**? They dig. Faster than before, sure. But they now pay rent for a task they used to do for free with their own tools.

Any resemblance to an existing technology would be, of course, purely coincidental.

## Should you throw away your shovel?

Let's be honest: no. The machine really does dig faster. Refusing to try it on principle is the best way to find yourself alone in your hole while the site next door moves forward.

But before signing the subscription, the training, and the energy contract, ask yourself the right questions:

- **What am I digging, exactly?** A hole dug faster is only worthwhile if the hole serves a purpose.
- **Do I still know how to dig without the machine?** The day it breaks down, the price triples, or the vendor shuts down, your arms will be your only fallback.
- **Am I the customer, or the loss leader?** Every hole you dig with the machine teaches the machine how to dig. One day, they might sell holes directly, without any digger.

## Conclusion

The new tools are impressive, and it would be foolish not to use them. But keep your old shovel in the back of the workshop, keep your arms in shape, and remember the one law that has never changed since the gold rush:

**When everyone is digging, sell shovels.**
