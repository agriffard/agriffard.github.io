The founding arcs of *Naruto* are genuine lessons in team 7 test. Let's look at what it teaches, then map it onto a software development team.

## The test that isn't what it seems

Kakashi presents two bells to three genin, with an implicit rule: whoever doesn't get one goes back to the academy. Naruto, Sasuke and Sakura fall into the trap and each attack on their own... and all get individually humiliated.

The real lesson: **the test measured cooperation, not individual performance**. None of the three could beat Kakashi alone, but together they would have had a chance. Kakashi says it explicitly:

> Those who break the rules are scum, but those who abandon their comrades are worse than scum.

## Dev team translation

**The bells are the sprint with seemingly individual goals** — assigned tickets. A team where everyone optimizes their own ticket without helping each other fails collectively: individual velocity doesn't ship a product.

**Kakashi is the tech lead evaluating the ability to collaborate, not to shine.** A brilliant dev who hoards information (Sasuke refusing to share his strategy) is more dangerous than a junior who asks questions.

**The artificially scarce resource** (2 bells for 3 people) mirrors situations where devs are pitted against each other — stack rankings, individual bonuses. It destroys exactly the behavior you want to see emerge.

**Naruto tied to the post** while the others are told not to feed him, and Sasuke and Sakura disobeying to share their food: that's the moment that validates the team. In dev terms, it's refusing to leave a colleague alone facing a production incident at 6 PM, even if "it's not my scope."

## Summary

| Naruto (Vol. 1) | Dev team |
|-----------------|----------|
| Bell test | Falsely individual goals; collaboration is the real criterion |
| Scarce resource (2 bells) | Internal competition = poison for cooperation |
| Feeding Naruto despite the ban | Solidarity > process when a teammate is struggling |

## Conclusion

The bell test fits in one Kakashi-style sentence: a team isn't three individuals working side by side to grab their own bell — it's an entity that protects its members, even when the rules say otherwise.
